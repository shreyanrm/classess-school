§ 01

What we are building

We are not building another learning-management system. We are building an agentic academic intelligence platform — a single, connected system that understands what is happening across an institution, identifies what needs attention, recommends what should happen next, coordinates the right people, and measures whether it worked. The platform does not wait to be asked. It observes, interprets, and acts.

A traditional LMS is a set of modules a school selects from: one for attendance, one for assignments, one for reports. The user is handed dashboards and left to find the problem, understand it, and decide what to do. Most people never get past "here is the data." Classess School inverts this. The modules still exist, but they are stitched into one continuous workflow — Plan → Teach → Observe → Assign → Assess → Evaluate → Support → Communicate → Improve — and intelligence runs across all of it.

Built for every board, and for many at once

The platform is board-agnostic. It is not built around a single curriculum. CBSE, ICSE, Cambridge, IB, state boards, and others are all first-class, and a single deployment can run several of them at the same time — a group with schools on different boards, or one school running multiple streams. The system adapts its curriculum structure, terminology, assessment patterns, report formats, and academic calendar to whatever the institution actually follows. Curriculum is ingested and mapped, not hard-coded.

Built for the full span of learning

The long-term horizon is the entire learning life — kindergarten through postgraduate. The current build targets K-12, but the architecture is not narrowed to children: the identity model, the academic graph, the intelligence, and the experience are designed to carry a learner across stages without fracturing. The interface changes as the learner grows; the understanding underneath keeps evolving with them.

What "AI-native" actually means here

AI-native is not an application with a chat button bolted on. It means intelligence is the substrate: content is generated rather than only stored, learners are understood rather than only recorded, experiences are composed per person rather than templated, and the system improves across every user and every event over time. Remove the AI from a conventional product and you still have a product. Remove it from this one and there is nothing underneath — because the intelligence is the product.

The platform's job is not to help users finish academic work. It is to understand what is happening, surface what matters, recommend the next best action, coordinate the right people, and measure whether it worked.that loop is the whole difference
§ 02

Guiding principles

Ten laws hold across every screen and every service. When a design decision is unclear, these settle it. They are the reason the product feels calm and certain while doing something genuinely complex underneath.

One screen, one intention, one next action

Navigation is journey-based, not module-based. No user needs to understand all the modules. Each role sees what to do now — not a wall of menus.

Explainable intelligence

Every recommendation shows why it appeared, the evidence behind it, a confidence level, who is responsible, and the consequence of ignoring it. Nothing is a black box.

Human authority

AI recommends, prepares, and assists. People make consequential decisions — grades, interventions, communications. The system never quietly acts on something that matters.

Calm on the surface, intensity underneath

The category is red-alert noise. We are the opposite: spacious, certain, quiet. The intelligence works hard; the interface never shouts. Calm is a deliberate signal, not an absence of power.

Progressive disclosure

Show the simple thing first. Advanced configuration and deep analytics appear only when asked for. Power is available, never imposed.

Board-agnostic & adaptive

The platform conforms to the institution's board, language, calendar, and assessment style — not the other way around. Curriculum is mapped, never assumed.

Evidence over assertion

Every important conclusion about a learner is linked to the evidence behind it, and no permanent judgement is made from a single interaction.

Offline & low-bandwidth

Attendance, lesson access, assignments, and basic evaluation keep working when the network does not. Connectivity is assumed to be imperfect.

Multilingual by design

Interface, conversation, content, and reports support local languages and code-switching while preserving subject terminology. Language is part of learning context.

Trust is a product layer

Consent, source attribution, data control, and auditability are built into the architecture from the first line — not added as a compliance afterthought.

The most important of these is the relationship between engagement and independence. The platform is designed to build durable academic habits — but it only ever engineers habit toward the behaviours that make a learner more independent: mastery, retrieval practice, independent attempts, consistency. The day a learner needs the platform less is the day it succeeded. Habit and integrity are the same loop, never opposing forces.

How the services are organised. App services are feature-based modules inside a single deployable with clean internal boundaries — not a tangled monolith, and not premature microservices. This keeps development fast now and lets us split any module into its own service later without re-architecting. Each app in the wider ecosystem owns its own operational database and runs independently; it shares what matters by emitting events to the platform intelligence layer, never by reaching into another app's tables. Apps share a platform, never tables.

Why identity sits at its own layer. Authentication and the behavioural data layer are deliberately kept separate. They have different security postures and different failure modes, and they should never share a blast radius. One layer answers who is this person; a different layer accumulates what we know about them. Keeping them apart is how disciplined platforms are built.

§ 04

The platform spine

Beneath the app sits a shared backbone of services. Classess School is the first product built on it, and everything built afterward inherits the same spine on day one. These are not School-specific — they are the reusable foundation of the whole ecosystem.

Identity

KGtoPG — the single front door

One canonical identity per human, for life, across every product. The moment anyone signs up to any product in the ecosystem, a KGtoPG account is created and used everywhere after — the same pattern as "sign in with Google," owned internally. There is no app-local signup; every app delegates here. Crucially, age and consent are established once, at this single point — which is what makes governance consistent instead of re-implemented (and re-broken) in every app. KGtoPG answers exactly one question: who is this person, and are they who they say.

Tech Identity service over OIDC / OAuth 2.0; phone-OTP-first (with Google / Apple / Microsoft and institutional SSO / SAML); role-based + attribute-based access control; session, device and risk management; full access history. Firebase Auth may serve as the underlying mechanism — the contract is what matters.
Conversational OS

Vidya — talk to do anything

Vidya is the conversational operating system that runs across every role. It is intention-primary: instead of hunting through menus, a user states what they want and Vidya summons the right surface, composes it on the fly, and operates it — a teacher saying "make a quiz on photosynthesis" gets a quiz-builder rendered in the conversation; a student saying "I don't get this" gets a tutoring surface. It is a workspace, not just a chat: conversation on one side, an editable canvas in the centre, sources and evidence alongside. It is role-shaped — the student's companion protects productive struggle and never just hands over answers; the teacher's executes and prepares; the parent's reports and reassures; the admin's commands the institution. The fixed pages remain as the stable spine; Vidya is the fast path over them.

Tech Orchestrator over the model router; generative-UI component library; multimodal input (text, voice, image, document, screen); persistent per-user memory; the permission ladder enforced in-conversation; a child-safety subsystem on every free-text surface (moderation, crisis detection and escalation to a responsible adult, no unmonitored channels).
Intelligence routing

The AI fabric & model router

Not "an LLM" — a fabric of models behind one router that chooses, per request, the right mind for the task by complexity, latency, cost, and the user's context. This single component is both quality control and margin control, and it is the most important piece of infrastructure in the company. It runs on two tracks that must never be conflated: Track 1 is best-in-class external models, chosen per feature; Track 2 is our own fine-tuned models and small specialised models running at the edge and offline — the long-term margin and the moat. The router exposes the slot for Track 2 now and fills it as those models come online, with no re-architecture.

Tech Self-hosted model gateway (LiteLLM) with per-feature routing, structured-output validation, retrieval, caching, and full cost/latency/quality observability (Langfuse). Frontier models for hard, rare reasoning; mid-tier for volume; small models at the edge for the high-frequency, latency-sensitive ocean of simple tasks.
Action layer

The capability registry & agent layer

Above the models sits the agent layer. Sub-agents are reasoning loops, not credentialed processes — they call named capabilities, which are governed server-side functions with narrowly scoped data access. An agent never holds a connection string and never writes raw queries; it can only invoke the capabilities it is permitted to. A permission ladder governs what any agent may do — Recommend → Prepare → Execute-with-permission → Safe-automatic — and anything that sends, submits, publishes, deletes, or charges requires explicit human approval. This is how "thousands of agents working for one user" is real and safe at once: one orchestrator commanding a registry of specialised, least-privilege capabilities.

Tech Capability registry with typed contracts and scoped access; agent registry recording each agent's purpose, permitted tools, and data boundaries; immutable audit trail; break-glass controls; human-approval workflows on consequential actions.
Interoperability

FLUID — be water

The platform embeds cleanly into whatever a school already runs, and outside tools plug into it — both directions, nothing breaks. A school on an existing LMS keeps it; Classess School can act as the intelligence layer on top, or take over, or simply exchange data. This is not magic — it is a set of open standards plus the platform's own contract, so onboarding the next institution or the next tool is configuration, not a project.

Tech LTI 1.3 / Advantage (tool launch, roster, grade passback) · OneRoster 1.2 (roster / course / grade exchange) · xAPI + Caliper (learning records) · QTI (assessment portability) · SCORM · Clever / ClassLink · Ed-Fi · CASE (competency frameworks) · the platform SDK · MCP for AI-tool interoperability.
The brain

The platform intelligence layer

This is the shared, event-driven intelligence and data layer that sits beneath every product. It is not a database — it is the system of record for who a learner is across the ecosystem and the system of intelligence for everything cross-context. Every app emits a stream of attributed events into it; it segregates, maps, and accumulates them into a unified profile, a knowledge graph, and a live feature store, and it serves back only governed, scoped views. Because the event history is immutable and replayable, when the models get smarter, the platform's understanding of every past learner improves with them — the intelligence compounds over time rather than starting fresh. Apps push a firehose of events up; they read only a controlled faucet of governed views down. No app ever bulk-reads the underlying store.

Tech Event ingestion + immutable event store; identity-separated profile store; knowledge graph; feature store; governed read APIs (scoped, consent- and purpose-gated). Apps integrate against this contract — the emission side is part of every app from line one.
The single most important rule for everyone building an app: emit a clean, attributed event for every meaningful action, from the first commit. The intelligence that comes later can only ever learn from what was recorded — what is not captured is lost forever.
§ 05

How the intelligence works

This is the part that makes the platform more than software. Six mechanisms work together: a curriculum ontology, a hyperlocalization layer, an evidence-based mastery model, a learning-gap engine, a generate-and-verify content pipeline, and a proactive loop that turns all of it into action. Running through every one of them is a single goal — to help a learner understand their own learning.

1 · The academic ontology — what everything means

Underneath the familiar names a teacher uses — chapters, topics — the platform holds a deep model of how academic knowledge connects: board → curriculum → grade → subject → unit → chapter → topic → prerequisite → learning outcome → competency → skill → question → resource. This is the ontology. It is what lets the system reason: this topic requires that earlier one; this question assesses that outcome; this gap traces back to a missing prerequisite. Because the platform is board-agnostic, the ontology also holds cross-board equivalence — the same concept mapped across CBSE, ICSE, Cambridge, IB, and state boards — plus competitive-exam mappings and school-defined local overlays. Curriculum is ingested from documents, publisher content, and standards feeds and mapped into this structure; it is never hard-coded to one board.

The principle behind it: similarity is not intelligence — relevance is, and relevance comes from context. A generic AI can find text that looks related. The platform knows what is academically relevant to this learner, on this board, at this grade, given what they have already mastered. The prerequisite map between topics is treated as an owned, validated artifact — proposed by the system, confirmed before it is trusted — because the mastery model, the gap engine, revision scheduling, and intervention all rest on it.

2 · Hyperlocalization — relevance, not translation

Ontology gives the platform understanding. Hyperlocalization gives it relevance. It is far more than language. The same concept is delivered differently depending on the institution's board, language, region, calendar, assessment patterns, and culture — fractions explained with locally familiar money or food portions, examples and terminology that fit the learner's world, report formats and exam patterns that match the board. During onboarding the institution's structure, policies, curriculum, and local context are captured and converted into platform configuration and AI behaviour, so the product becomes institution-specific intelligence rather than a one-size-fits-all tool.

3 · The mastery model — two 80%s are not equal

The platform never reduces a learner to an average score. A score earned with help, on easy questions, long ago, is not the same as a score earned independently, on hard questions, recently, and repeated. So mastery is computed as a weighting of several dimensions:

Mastery = Performance × Reliability × Independence
× Difficulty × Recency × Consistency

The Independence dimension is the one almost no system tracks, and it is decisive: it separates what a learner can do alone from what they can only do with support. A learner scoring 90% with heavy hints and 62% unaided is not "developing at 76%" — they are support-dependent, and the platform says so. Learners never see the raw formula; they see plain language — "you understand this with guidance," "you can solve familiar questions independently," "you previously mastered this; revision is now due." This model feeds the knowledge profile, the gap engine, revision planning, and every intervention.

4 · The learning-gap engine — naming what is actually wrong

A low score is a symptom, not a diagnosis. The platform distinguishes ten kinds of gap, because each needs a different response:

  • Prerequisite — an earlier concept is weak
  • Conceptual — the underlying idea isn't understood
  • Procedural — the idea is clear but the steps fail
  • Application — fine on familiar questions, lost on new ones
  • Retention — understood once, now forgotten
  • Language — the concept is fine; the wording is the barrier
  • Accuracy — method is right, careless errors recur
  • Speed — correct, but not within the time
  • Confidence — knows it, avoids or second-guesses
  • Support-dependency — strong with help, weak alone

Detection is a disciplined process, not a guess — and a gap is never confirmed from a single bad score:

The thread through all of it — metacognition

Every mechanism above points at one outcome: helping a learner see and regulate their own thinking. The mastery model shows them what they truly own versus what they only borrow with help. The gap engine names why something is hard, not just that it is. Confidence check-ins, reflection prompts, and an "I think I'm right" re-grade path build self-assessment. The knowledge profile is queryable — a learner can ask what they are weakest at and what to do next. The platform's deepest job is not to deliver answers; it is to make a learner a better, more independent judge of their own understanding.

§ 06

Applications & roles

Four role-experiences run on one platform: Admin, Teacher, Student, and Parent. They are not four separate products — they are four views of the same system, each showing only what that person needs, governed by permissions. The institution's many leadership and support positions live as permission-based roles inside the Admin experience; the many kinds of educator live inside the Teacher experience.

Admin

The institution's control surface — owner, principal, coordinator, HOD, examination, student-support, IT. Each sees a different, scoped slice. Manage by exception: the platform surfaces what needs attention; the admin approves and steers.

Teacher

The friction killer — school teacher, faculty, tutor, mentor, special educator. The platform prepares the work; the teacher reviews and decides. Most actions take three steps or fewer.

Student

The learner — never overwhelmed with administration or analytics, always shown the next step and why. The experience adapts to age and stage.

Parent

Meaningful visibility and a sense of pride and partnership — not a surveillance feed. Action, explanation, and the child's progress, in the parent's own language.

One person can hold several roles — a teacher who is also a parent, a principal who is also a management member — under one identity, switching context cleanly. And whatever the role, the home is the same shape: a small set of surfaces rather than a wall of modules — Today (what needs attention now), My Work (tasks and approvals), Ask (the conversational companion), People & Classes, Insights (explanations and recommended actions), and Apps (the deeper modules, when needed).

§ 07

The feature catalogue

This is the full capability surface, grouped by domain. For each, what it does, how it functions, and the tools that build it. This is the complete picture of the system — not a reduced first version. The build sequences these features onto a complete foundation; it does not strip them away.

1 · Institutional setup, governance & hyperlocalization

Every institution is different — a single school, a multi-campus group, a franchise, an NGO programme, a government network. The platform models all of them without forcing them into one rigid shape.

Institutional Blueprint wizard

The institution completes a guided setup interview rather than hundreds of configuration fields.

How: the wizard captures organisation structure, board and curriculum, academic year, working days and holidays, assessment and timetable policies, approval workflows, and communication rules, then generates the recommended structure, roles, and policies for approval. From this it builds a digital twin of how the institution operates.

Organisation hierarchy & relationship graph

A configurable hierarchy — group → region → campus → school → department → grade → section — plus a relationship graph for ownership, management, affiliation, and funding that a simple tree cannot express.

How: nodes are configurable, not hard-coded levels, so an independent school, a group, a franchise, an NGO with sponsored schools, and a government district-and-block structure all fit. A single school can simultaneously belong to a group, sit in a government district, and be funded by an external programme — many-to-many relationships, each scoped and time-bound.

Policy inheritance & governance

Policies flow down the hierarchy and can be inherited, overridden, or locked at each level.

How: a group sets a standard; one school inherits it, another is permitted a variation, a third is centrally locked. The same model governs curriculum, assessment, grading, attendance, communication, AI usage, and data retention — with versioning, effective dates, and audit trails. Hyperlocalization sits here too: board terminology, local language, regional calendars, and local exam formats become live configuration.

Builds with hierarchy + relationship-graph data model in PostgreSQL · policy engine with inheritance and versioning · the conversational wizard over Vidya · multi-tenancy with logical isolation (dedicated environments for large deployments).

2 · Identity, roles, access & consent

One identity per person, many possible roles, access scoped to exactly what each role legitimately needs.

Single identity, single sign-on

Every user signs in once through the identity layer and carries one canonical identity everywhere.

How: the SSO front door supports phone-OTP-first plus Google / Apple / Microsoft and institutional SSO, and provisions the identity automatically on first signup. One person can hold multiple roles and switch between them without separate accounts.

Role- and attribute-based access

What a user can do is governed by role; where and for whom is governed by attributes.

How: a coordinator can edit, but only for their assigned grade; a support role sees only referred students; a donor view sees aggregated outcomes but no student identity. Delegated, temporary, and substitute access are first-class, and role removal is immediate when employment or enrolment changes.

Consent as a primitive

Consent for AI use, camera, microphone, recordings, and data sharing is captured, scoped, and revocable.

How: consent is established at the identity layer and travels with the data, gating what each role and each cross-context read may use. This is built in from the first line rather than added later, and underpins the platform's trust posture.

Builds with the identity layer (OIDC / OAuth 2.0 / SAML) · RBAC + ABAC policy enforcement at the gateway · consent and permission service · full access history and audit.

3 · Academic structure & ontology

The curriculum backbone that everything academic hangs from — built once, board-agnostic, and reusable across the whole platform.

Board mapping & curriculum ingestion

Any board's curriculum is ingested and mapped into the ontology — subjects, units, chapters, topics, outcomes.

How: curriculum comes from documents, standards feeds, and publisher content and is mapped to the structure described in §05, with cross-board equivalence so the same concept is recognised across boards. Teachers work with familiar chapter and topic names while the deeper relationships are managed underneath.

Ontology steward

The system proposes mappings, flags duplicates, detects missing prerequisites, and recommends updates — for academic approval.

How: the steward keeps the curriculum graph clean and current as content grows, but proposed changes are confirmed by a human before they are trusted. Curriculum is versioned, and learning-outcome coverage is tracked.

Builds with the academic ontology + prerequisite graph (PostgreSQL + graph relationships, pgvector for semantic matching) · ingestion pipeline with document understanding · CASE for competency-framework exchange.

4 · Academic calendar & the dynamic smart timetable

Not a document prepared once a term — a continuously optimised operational layer that keeps teaching going when the day changes.

Automatic timetable generation

The system generates timetable options scored for compliance, workload balance, and academic quality, and explains why a recommended version is best.

How: a constraint engine classifies every rule as hard (a teacher cannot be in two rooms at once), soft (maths is better earlier), or contextual (more revision near exams), then produces alternatives — best academic balance, best workload balance, best resource use — for an authorised human to compare and approve.

Academic continuity & the substitution ladder

When a teacher becomes unavailable, the platform doesn't just mark an absence — it keeps the class going from where the previous teacher left off.

How: a configurable ladder searches for the best cover, in order, and picks up the lesson at the right point so students lose no continuity.

Period swapping & pacing protection

The engine can exchange periods across the week and protects syllabus completion.

How: it tracks planned vs. delivered periods and instructional time lost, and when a subject falls behind it recommends recovery — added periods, revision blocks, reallocated slots — so timetable decisions protect academic progress, not just fill slots. Low-risk changes can be automated within policy; high-impact changes require approval.

Builds with a constraint solver + scheduling service · the workflow engine for approvals and notifications · integration with attendance and leave so an absence triggers the ladder automatically.

5 · Content repository & resources

One place where a teacher finds approved school content, publisher resources, past materials that worked, and AI-generated activities.

Unified, tagged content library

Institution content, personal teacher libraries, and publisher collections, all tagged to the ontology and learning outcomes.

How: uploaded material passes through OCR, transcription, and document understanding; videos gain searchable transcripts and chapters; everything carries versioning, approval status, and licence metadata, and duplicates are detected. A teacher picks a topic and sees everything relevant in one view.

Generated supporting material

Summaries, worksheets, mind maps, and interactive presentations generated on demand for any topic.

How: the content engine produces these grounded on the curriculum and verified before use, and they flow straight into lessons and student modules. Mind maps render topics as visual flow with auto-generated assessment; presentations carry interactive elements and embedded media.

Builds with object storage + CDN · OCR / transcription / document-understanding pipeline · the content engine and verification substrate · pgvector for semantic search across resources.

6 · Teacher planning & instruction design

The teacher starts from "what am I teaching today," and the platform prepares the lesson, the likely misconceptions, the checks for understanding, and the differentiation.

Adaptive lesson & course planning

Annual, unit, weekly, and daily plans — generated from the teacher's preferences and instructional model, and adjusted day to day.

How: plans are generated against curriculum requirements and a chosen instructional model, then the next day's plan automatically adapts to the previous day's completion, the engagement observed, and how students performed on those topics — keeping productivity high while staying on track for syllabus completion.

Differentiation & readiness-aware planning

Plans account for student readiness, prior evidence, available time, and resources.

How: the planner draws on the mastery model and gap data to suggest differentiated paths and prerequisite checks, and routes plans for approval by a coordinator or head where required. An automatic teacher diary records what was actually taught versus planned.

Builds with the content engine + ontology · mastery and gap data from the intelligence layer · reusable institutional templates · approval workflow.

7 · Classroom delivery, live class & the interactive board

The teacher launches the day's period from the timetable — content, attendance, and assessment in one place — rather than hunting across modules. Physical, online, and hybrid are all supported.

The interactive teaching board

An infinite canvas with subject-aware teaching content — write, draw, and annotate anywhere, with 3D explorable models, simulations, and animated explanations on the board.

How: a fast native ink layer floats over hardware-accelerated interactive content sharing one camera, with multiple board themes, full drawing and shape tools, image and document sharing, and the ability to switch between live interaction and annotating a frozen frame. Content is generated and verified for the topic being taught that session.

Live polls, quizzes & the in-class check

Launch a poll or a quiz mid-lesson and see grasp immediately — including a device-free mode.

How: where students have devices, they answer online; where they do not, each student holds a unique response card and the teacher photographs the room for instant on-the-spot evaluation and a live leaderboard. Quizzes link to the day's lesson so the teacher can quantify what was actually grasped.

Engagement & attention signals

§ 09

The AI layer in depth

The AI is not one model behind a chat box. It is a fabric of many minds, a per-learner intelligence layer, a generate-and-verify content pipeline, and a governed agent system — routed and observed so quality stays high and cost stays controlled.

Many minds, one router

The fabric uses different models for different work, chosen per request:

Frontier models

The hard, rare, high-stakes work — generating a genuinely novel problem, diagnosing a subtle misconception, deep reasoning. Used sparingly, where quality is decisive.

Mid-tier models

The workhorse volume — explanations, feedback, summaries.

Small specialised models

The high-frequency, latency- and cost-sensitive ocean — quick classification, routing, intent detection, simple checks. This is where margin is won.

Our own fine-tuned models

The moat — trained on the platform's own academic data, ontology, and rubrics; tuned to the curricula, languages, and gap taxonomy the platform serves. Pedagogy-tuned models outperform raw frontier models at teaching.

This is the two-track strategy in practice: Track 1 (external models) carries the load today; Track 2 (our own models and edge / offline small models) fills in over time for margin and defensibility. The router decides per request by complexity, latency, cost, and context — and the slot for Track 2 exists from the start, so adding our own models requires no re-architecture.

A mind for every learner

Personalisation does not mean a separate model per student — that would be wasteful and pointless. It means a persistent per-learner intelligence layer — the knowledge profile, behavioural signals, learning style, history — that every model call is conditioned on. The model is shared; the context is uniquely theirs. The same model, fed a learner's cognitive fingerprint, behaves like a tutor that has known only them for years. The artifact that makes this real is a learned representation of how that specific learner thinks, drawn from the event history.

Generate, verify, and the agent system

Content is generated against the ontology and verified before serving, as in §05 — deterministic checks where possible, a second-model cross-check, and a confidence gate that refuses unverified work. Above the models, the orchestrator interprets intent, pulls context, decides which capability and which model to invoke, acts within the permission ladder, verifies, and remembers. Capabilities are governed, least-privilege functions; agents never hold credentials or touch data directly. Everything is observed — cost, latency, and quality per route — because without that, AI spend silently erodes margin. Removing any one of these layers collapses the system back into ordinary software; their interdependence is what makes it AI-native.

§ 10

Data & event architecture

This is the part that makes the platform an ecosystem rather than a folder of apps, and it rests on one rule that every developer must internalise. Each app owns its own operational data and runs independently. It joins the ecosystem by doing three things, and only three.

  1. Authenticate through the identity layer — one sign-in, one canonical identity, valid everywhere.
  2. Emit its data upward as attributed events — a firehose of everything meaningful, each event stamped with which app, which canonical user, which purpose, which consent, and what type. The app can emit bluntly; the contract makes it clean on arrival.
  3. Read only governed, scoped views downward — a controlled faucet, never bulk access to the underlying store.

The direction of flow is the safety rule. App-to-platform is a firehose — push everything, raw, attributed. Platform-to-app is a faucet — only the scoped views the contract grants. No app ever bulk-reads the canonical store. This single asymmetry gives the ecosystem three things at once: independence (an app keeps running if the platform is briefly unreachable), a clean security boundary (an app sees only its own slice, never across the platform), and durable value (the data stays with the platform; an app that leaves loses access while the accumulated intelligence remains).

Why this must be in place from line one. Because the event history is immutable and replayable, the intelligence layer can be enriched at any time and re-process the whole stream — so understanding of every past learner improves as the models improve. But that only works if clean, attributed events were emitted in the first place. An app that merely writes to its operational tables and moves on cannot be retrofitted later without losing the behavioural history forever. The emission side is therefore part of every app from the first commit; the heavier intelligence that consumes it can mature alongside, but the contract cannot be deferred.

Retention and deletion are clean by design. Raw personal data is the learner's and is removed on deletion; de-identified, aggregated learning — the patterns dissolved into the model across many learners — is retained, because the link to the individual is severed and their contribution already lives in aggregate. Every derived insight carries source lineage and is recalculated when permissions change. No personal data is ever sold, and private academic data is never used for advertising.

§ 12

How it all interconnects

Every part of this document is one system. A person signs in once and is known everywhere. They act inside an app. The app emits what happened. The intelligence layer learns from it and hands back understanding. That understanding becomes a recommendation, a human approves it, the app carries it out, and the outcome flows back in to sharpen the next one. That single circuit is the entire platform.

One event, traced all the way through

Follow a single moment. A student submits a worksheet. The app stores it and emits an attributed event. The evaluation engine scores it against the rubric, separates a wrong answer from an incomplete one from a misunderstood one, and attaches a confidence level. The intelligence layer records the evidence, updates mastery on the affected topics — weighting that the work was unaided and recent — and the gap engine notices the same procedural slip across several students. The proactive loop raises one recommendation to the teacher: these eight students share this specific gap; here is a fifteen-minute activity and a practice set. The teacher approves. The app assigns the work and tells the student what to do next and why. The parent sees, in their language, that the school spotted something and acted. Days later the reassessment flows back in, mastery updates on real evidence, and the next recommendation is a little better. No part of this is a separate product — it is one circuit turning once.

This is why the foundation cannot be skipped and the event contract cannot be deferred. Remove identity and nothing is connected. Remove the event emission and the intelligence has nothing to learn from. Remove verification and the content cannot be trusted. Remove the permission ladder and the system cannot be allowed to act. Each layer depends on the others; together they are an academic intelligence platform, and apart they are ordinary software.

§ 13

Build approach

One principle governs the order of work: sequence the features, never the foundation. The platform spine is built complete and correct from the start, even where a consumer for it does not yet exist. Features are then layered onto that stable base in a deliberate order. The capability surface in this document is the full target, not a reduced first version — the phases are about sequence, not subtraction.

Phase 0

Foundation — the platform spine

Identity, consent, and roles. The canonical data model and the event contract. The academic ontology and prerequisite graph. The AI fabric and model router. The capability registry skeleton. The API gateway. The unified home and the workflow engine that powers every proactive behaviour. The interoperability contract surface.

Why first: nothing below works without it. This is the highest-stakes work in the system; it is built to be complete and stable, because everything else rests on it — and because the event contract, once live, lets all later intelligence learn retroactively from day one.

Phase 1

The academic loop — School becomes real

The dynamic timetable and academic continuity. The content and resource layer. Teacher planning. Classroom delivery. Attendance intelligence. Assignments. Assessment architecture. The evaluation engine. Learn and practice. Revision and mocks.

Why next: this is the complete teaching-and-learning cycle — the thing an institution actually runs on every day, and the thing that proves the platform in real classrooms.

Phase 2

Intelligence & relationships

The unified learner profile and evidence graph. The conversational companion across every role. The parent experience. The communication hub. Parent–teacher partnership. The academic intelligence and proactive layer at full strength. Teacher development and continuity.

Why here: these capabilities are richest once real academic activity is flowing and the intelligence layer has evidence to reason over. They turn a working system into an intelligent one.

Phase 3

Ecosystem scale

Multi-tenancy across school, group, franchise, programme, and network. The full organisational hierarchy and relationship graph with programme and funding overlays. The partner and content marketplace. Group, district, and network control centres. Advanced operations for the agent and intelligence layers.

Why last: scale is layered on once the single-institution product is excellent. Correct architecture stretched too thin too early is the failure mode we avoid; depth first, breadth after.

Across all four phases, one discipline holds the whole build together, and it is worth stating plainly one last time: every app emits clean, attributed events from the first commit. The features can arrive in sequence and the heavier intelligence can mature over time — but the contract that makes all of it possible is in place from line one, because the understanding the platform will one day have can only ever be built from what was recorded along the way.

Build the foundation as if everything depends on it, because everything does. Sequence the features with patience. Hold the standard on every screen. this is the one we don't get wrong
